# How to Price and Budget Your Custom Software Project

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** custom software pricing, offshore software budget, software development cost

The most frustrating interaction a CEO can have is calling a custom software development agency and asking: *"I want to build an Uber for Dog Walking. How much will it cost?"*

The agency representative usually replies: *"Well, it depends. It could be $50,000, or it could be $500,000."*

The CEO thinks the agency is being evasive. But the agency is actually being honest. Asking "How much does software cost?" is exactly like asking "How much does a house cost?" A tiny wooden shack and a 50-room mansion are both "houses," but the engineering required is vastly different. 

If you are planning to outsource a custom software project to an offshore development center, you must understand how software is priced, and how to control the budget.

## The Three Pricing Models
When you sign a contract with an offshore agency, you will usually choose between three financial models.

### 1. Fixed Price (The Trap)
The agency promises to build the entire app for exactly $100,000, delivered in exactly four months. 
CEOs love Fixed Price because it feels safe. It is actually the most dangerous model. 
To guarantee a Fixed Price, the agency must write a 100-page "Scope of Work" document detailing every single button. If you change your mind in Month 2 and want to add an AI feature, the agency will say, *"No, that isn't in the contract. We have to sign a $20,000 Change Order."* 
Fixed Price projects mathematically incentivize the agency to cut corners to protect their profit margin. The software is delivered on time, but it is usually rigid and poorly coded.

### 2. Time and Materials (The Standard)
You pay the agency for the exact hours the developers work. If the Senior Backend Developer costs $50/hour, and they work 100 hours this month, you pay $5,000. 
This is the standard model for Agile development. It gives you infinite flexibility. If you want to pivot the entire product in Month 3, you just tell the team to pivot. 
The downside? The CEO absorbs all the financial risk. If the project takes longer than expected, the budget expands. 

### 3. Dedicated Team (The Enterprise Choice)
Instead of paying per hour or per project, you "rent" an entire team. You pay a flat monthly retainer (e.g., $35,000/month) for a dedicated squad: 1 Tech Lead, 2 Frontend Developers, 1 Backend Developer, and 1 QA Tester. 
These engineers act as your full-time remote employees. This is the most cost-effective and scalable model for long-term, massive enterprise projects. 

## The Hidden Costs of Software
When budgeting for custom software, do not just calculate the developer hours. You must budget for the "Invisible Third."

* **Cloud Hosting (AWS/Google):** The software has to live somewhere. For an enterprise app, expect to pay $500 to $2,000+ a month just to rent the servers. 
* **Third-Party APIs:** If your app sends SMS text messages (Twilio) or processes credit cards (Stripe), you have to pay those companies a tiny fee for every transaction. 
* **Ongoing Maintenance:** Software is not a house; it is a living organism. When Apple releases iOS 20 next year, your app might break. You must budget roughly 15% to 20% of the initial build cost per year purely for continuous maintenance and security patches. 

The best way to control your software budget is to aggressively restrict the initial scope. Build the absolute bare-minimum MVP (Minimum Viable Product). Launch it to your users, figure out exactly what they actually want, and then deploy the rest of your budget building those specific features.
