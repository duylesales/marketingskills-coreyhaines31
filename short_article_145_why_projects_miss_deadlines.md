# Why Software Projects Miss Deadlines (And How to Fix It)

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** software project delays, agile software delivery, outsourcing timeline 

According to industry reports, nearly 60% of all custom software projects miss their original deadline. 

When a project is six weeks late, executives usually blame the developers. *"They are writing code too slowly!"* or *"The offshore agency lied to us about the timeline!"*

While bad developers certainly exist, the vast majority of software delays have absolutely nothing to do with typing speed. Software projects almost always derail due to systemic failures in management and scope. If you are outsourcing a major application, here are the three hidden reasons your project will miss its deadline, and exactly how to fix them.

## 1. Scope Creep (The Silent Killer)
This is the number one cause of missed deadlines globally. 

The project starts with a strict agreement to build an MVP with 10 features. The offshore agency estimates it will take exactly 3 months. 

In Month 2, the CEO has a brilliant idea in the shower. They email the developers: *"Hey, can we quickly add a feature where users can log in using their Apple ID instead of just email?"*
The developer, wanting to be helpful, says, *"Sure, that shouldn't take too long."* 

It actually takes four days to configure the Apple API securely. Over the next month, the CEO asks for five more "tiny" features. Suddenly, 20 days of extra work have been stealthily added to the project. The 3-month deadline is shattered.

**The Fix:** You must enforce ruthless Agile discipline. Once a 2-week "Sprint" begins, the scope is mathematically locked. If the CEO wants a new Apple Login feature, the Scrum Master must put it in the "Backlog" to be evaluated for the next sprint. Every new feature added must push the final deadline back. 

## 2. "The Final 10%" Illusion
In software development, the first 90% of the code takes 90% of the time. The last 10% of the code also takes 90% of the time. 

If you are building an e-commerce app, building the shopping cart and the product pages goes incredibly fast. The executives look at the demo and think, *"Wow, the app is basically done!"*

They don't realize the final 10% is the agonizing, invisible work. It is configuring the SSL security certificates. It is making sure the app doesn't crash on an obscure 5-year-old Android phone. It is fixing edge-case bugs in the payment gateway. 

**The Fix:** Stop estimating based on visual progress. Do not set a launch date based on when the app "looks" finished. You must allocate a massive, dedicated block of time (usually 20% of the entire project timeline) purely for QA testing, bug fixing, and server configuration before the official deadline. 

## 3. The "Waiting on the Client" Bottleneck
Offshore agencies often take the blame for delays that are entirely the client's fault. 

The offshore team builds a new dashboard, but they cannot merge the code to the live server until the client's internal Product Owner officially reviews and approves it. The Product Owner is busy with other meetings and doesn't test the dashboard for five days. The developers are blocked. Five days of momentum are permanently lost.

**The Fix:** Outsourcing is a partnership, not a vending machine. If you hire an offshore development center, your internal stakeholders must commit to a Service Level Agreement (SLA) for approvals. If the offshore team submits code for review, the internal team must legally commit to reviewing it within 24 hours, ensuring the development engine never stalls.
