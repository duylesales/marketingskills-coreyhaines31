# Why Software Engineers Hate Estimating Time (And How to Fix It)

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** agile software estimation, IT outsourcing timelines, story points vs hours

You are building a custom CRM with an offshore development agency. You have a meeting with the Lead Developer and ask a very simple question: 
*"How long will it take to build the new Email Marketing feature?"*

The developer squirms. They look at the ceiling. They hesitate. Finally, they say, *"Maybe... three weeks? But it depends."*

Business executives find this infuriating. If you ask a construction worker how long it takes to build a brick wall, they can tell you exactly how many hours it will take. Why can't highly paid software engineers estimate their own work?

Here is the secret: Software engineering is not construction; it is invention. You cannot accurately estimate how long it takes to invent something that does not exist yet. Here is why estimates fail, and how modern Agile teams solve this problem using **Story Points**.

## The Fallacy of Hourly Estimation
If you ask an offshore developer to estimate a feature in "Hours," they are guessing. 

The developer thinks: *"Building the email button will take 2 hours. Connecting the database will take 8 hours. So, 10 hours total."*

But when they start coding, they discover a hidden variable. The specific third-party Email API they are trying to connect to has broken documentation. A task that should have taken 8 hours now takes 40 hours of frustrating debugging. The developer misses the deadline, the client is furious, and trust is destroyed. 

Because developers are terrified of this happening, they start "padding" their estimates. If they think a feature takes 10 hours, they will quote you 30 hours, just to be safe. You end up paying for a massive, artificial buffer.

## The Solution: Story Points (Relative Estimation)
To fix this, elite offshore development teams refuse to estimate in hours. They use an Agile concept called **Story Points**.

Story Points do not measure time. They measure **Complexity and Effort**. 

Imagine you ask a developer to estimate the time it takes to run a mile. The developer cannot give you an exact time because they don't know the terrain. Is the mile uphill? Is it muddy? Is it raining?

Instead, you ask the developer to compare the effort. 
* You declare that building a simple "Login Page" is a **1-Point Task** (the baseline). 
* You ask the developer: *"Compared to the Login Page, how complex is the new Email Marketing feature?"*
* The developer answers: *"The Email feature is about five times more complex than a Login Page. Let's call it a **5-Point Task**."*

The developer is highly accurate at relative comparison, even if they don't know exactly how many hours it will take.

## Calculating "Velocity"
Once the offshore team starts working in Story Points, magic happens. 

In Sprint 1 (a two-week period), the team completes exactly 20 Story Points worth of work. 
In Sprint 2, they complete 22 Story Points. 
In Sprint 3, they complete 19 Story Points. 

You now have a mathematical average: **The team's "Velocity" is 20 Points per Sprint.**

If your remaining project backlog has 100 points left in it, you simply divide 100 by 20. You now know, with mathematical certainty, that the project will be finished in exactly 5 Sprints (10 weeks). 

You arrived at a hyper-accurate timeline without ever forcing a single developer to guess how many hours a task would take. By embracing Story Points and Velocity, you eliminate the friction between business expectations and engineering reality.
