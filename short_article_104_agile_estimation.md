# Agile Software Estimation: Why Developers Struggle to Give Exact Quotes

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** software estimation, Agile software development, custom software pricing

One of the most frustrating moments for a business leader is asking a software team for a price quote. 

The CEO asks, "How much will it cost to build this new mobile app?" 
The Lead Developer shifts uncomfortably and says, "Well, it could take three months, or maybe six months. It depends."

To a CEO who buys raw materials and manufacturing equipment at exact, fixed prices every day, this sounds like incompetence or a scam. Why can't software engineers just give an exact, fixed price for a project?

The answer lies in the fundamental nature of custom software engineering. 

## Software is Not a House; It is an Expedition
The most common analogy used by clients is construction. "If I want to build a 3-bedroom house, a contractor can tell me exactly how many bricks it needs and exactly what it will cost. Why can't you do that for my app?"

This analogy is flawed. Building a house is a known, repeatable process. Humans have built millions of identical houses. 

Building *custom* software is not like building a house; it is like paying an expedition team to map an uncharted jungle. You know you want to reach the river on the other side, but you don't know if there is a massive swamp, a mountain, or hostile terrain in the middle until you start hacking through the vines. 

## The Illusion of the Fixed-Bid Contract
Some desperate outsourcing agencies *will* give you a fixed price. They will say, "We will build the app for exactly $100,000 in exactly 4 months."

This is known as a Fixed-Bid contract, and it is a trap. 
Because the agency knows they will lose money if they encounter "swamps" in the code, they will cut every possible corner to hit the deadline. They will write terrible, insecure code, skip QA testing, and refuse to accommodate any changes. 
If you realize in month two that you need to change a feature, the agency will point to the contract and say, "That wasn't in the original 100-page spec document. We won't do it."

## How Agile Solves Estimation
Instead of demanding a fake, rigid guarantee, modern software teams use **Agile Estimation**. 

Agile teams do not estimate time in "hours" or "dollars." They estimate complexity using a system called "Story Points." 
* A simple login screen might be assigned a complexity of **2 Points**.
* A complex third-party payment integration might be assigned a complexity of **8 Points**.

The team then starts working in 2-week "Sprints." After the first few sprints, a reliable metric emerges called **Velocity**. The team realizes they are consistently completing about 40 Story Points every two weeks. 

## Forecasting, Not Promising
Once the team knows their Velocity, estimation becomes a mathematical forecast, much like predicting the weather. 
The Product Manager can look at the remaining backlog of features (let's say there are 200 Story Points left) and do the math. "At a velocity of 40 points per sprint, we will be finished in exactly 5 sprints (10 weeks)."

This Agile approach is why hiring a dedicated offshore team on a monthly retainer is vastly superior to project-based fixed-bids. You pay for the team's continuous Velocity. If you want to add a new feature, you simply swap it into the backlog. You retain total control over the budget and the direction of the expedition, ensuring you get exactly what you need, rather than what was written in an outdated contract six months ago.
