# How to Outsource Game Development Successfully

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** outsource game development, offshore unity developers, game studio outsourcing

The global video game industry generates more revenue than the film and music industries combined. As the demand for massive, high-fidelity games (and "gamified" corporate apps) skyrockets, even the biggest "AAA" game studios in the world cannot hire enough local talent to hit their launch dates.

As a result, almost every major game release today heavily relies on **Offshore Game Development**. 

However, outsourcing game development is completely different from outsourcing a B2B SaaS web app. You are not just building functional logic; you are building art, physics, and human emotion. If managed incorrectly, the offshore code will clash with the art, resulting in a buggy, unplayable mess. Here is how to outsource game development safely.

## 1. Co-Development vs. Asset Creation
You must decide exactly *what* you are outsourcing.

**Asset Creation:** This is the easiest form of outsourcing. You hire an offshore studio in Vietnam to create thousands of 3D models (trees, guns, cars, character skins). The offshore team does not write code; they just deliver beautiful art files, which your internal US team imports into the game engine. 
**Co-Development:** This is much harder. You hire offshore Unity or Unreal Engine C++ developers to actually write the game code (e.g., programming the enemy AI, building the multiplayer matchmaking system, or optimizing the frame rate). 

If you are doing Co-Development, your internal Technical Director must enforce ruthless coding standards, or the offshore code will not compile with your internal code.

## 2. Choosing the Right Engine (Unity vs. Unreal)
You cannot hire a "generic" software agency to build a video game. They must be specialists in a specific Game Engine. 

* **Unity (C#):** Ideal for mobile games, 2D platformers, and Indie games. It is highly flexible and has a massive pool of offshore talent available.
* **Unreal Engine (C++):** Required for hyper-realistic 3D graphics, massive open-world AAA games, and high-end console development. Unreal developers are rarer and more expensive, but the visual output is unparalleled.

Do not allow an offshore agency to "learn the engine on the job." You must verify their portfolio to ensure they have shipped successful titles on your specific engine.

## 3. The Nightmare of "Game Feel"
In business software, a button either works or it doesn't. 
In a video game, making a character jump isn't just about the code; it's about the "Game Feel." Does the jump feel heavy? Is the animation snappy? Does the camera shake slightly when they land? 

"Game Feel" is incredibly difficult to communicate across a language and cultural barrier via Jira tickets. 

To solve this, you must enforce a **Rapid Prototyping loop**. You do not wait two weeks for the offshore team to finish a feature. You require daily "Playable Builds." Every single day, the offshore team sends a compiled version of the game. Your internal Game Designer plays it with a controller, records a video of themselves playing it, and sends the video back to the offshore team with exact time-stamped feedback (*"At 0:14, the jump feels too floaty. Increase gravity by 15%."*).

## 4. IP and Source Control Security
Video game source code and unreleased 3D assets are highly targeted by hackers and leakers. 

You must mandate strict security. The offshore team must use secure Version Control (like Perforce, which handles massive art files much better than standard Git). The offshore developers must sign strict NDAs, and their network must be audited to ensure unreleased game assets cannot be leaked to Reddit or Discord. 

By defining the scope strictly, utilizing daily playable builds, and partnering with specialized game dev studios, you can scale your production globally and hit your launch dates without sacrificing the magic of the game.
