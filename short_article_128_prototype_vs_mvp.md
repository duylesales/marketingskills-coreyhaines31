# The Difference Between a Software Prototype and an MVP

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** MVP software development, software prototype, proof of concept

There is a fatal misunderstanding in the software development industry that causes startups and enterprise innovation labs to burn millions of dollars: confusing a **Prototype** with a **Minimum Viable Product (MVP)**.

Executives often use these terms interchangeably. They will tell an offshore development team, *"Build me a quick prototype, but make sure it can handle 10,000 users so we can sell it."*

That is a paradox. If it can handle 10,000 paying users securely, it is not a prototype. It is a massive, expensive piece of production software. 

If you do not strictly define the difference between these two phases before you sign an outsourcing contract, your project will fail. Here is the technical and business difference between a Prototype and an MVP.

## The Prototype: The Illusion of Software
The sole purpose of a Prototype is to answer one question: *Will the user actually click this button?*

A Prototype is an illusion. It is a movie set. It looks like a real application on the screen, but if you look behind the screen, there is no database, no security, and no backend logic. 
In many cases, a Prototype isn't even written in code. It is a clickable, high-fidelity design file built in Figma. The user taps a button on their phone, and the screen changes, but it is just a slide presentation simulating an app.

* **Cost:** $2,000 – $10,000
* **Timeline:** 1 to 3 weeks
* **Goal:** Put it in front of 5 potential customers and investors to see if they understand the concept. 
* **The Rule:** You cannot sell a Prototype. When you are done testing it, you literally throw the files in the trash.

## The MVP: The Smallest Real Product
The MVP (Minimum Viable Product) is actual, functional, secure software. 

The word "Minimum" does not mean "broken" or "buggy." It means the scope of features is brutally restricted. 
If your grand vision is to build an app that orders food, tracks the delivery via GPS, offers loyalty points, and integrates with Facebook, your MVP must strip away 80% of that.

Your MVP only orders the food and charges the credit card. That is it. 

However, because it is charging a real credit card, the backend architecture must be perfect. The database must be secure. The AWS servers must be scalable. The MVP is the concrete foundation upon which you will build the rest of the features over the next five years.

* **Cost:** $30,000 – $100,000+
* **Timeline:** 2 to 4 months
* **Goal:** Launch it to 500 real paying customers to prove the business model actually generates revenue.
* **The Rule:** You do not throw the MVP away. You continually update and expand its codebase.

## The "Frankenstein" Mistake
The most common disaster in software outsourcing occurs when a CEO tries to turn a Prototype into an MVP without starting over. 

They hire a cheap freelancer to build a quick, messy, duct-taped Prototype to show investors. The investors love it and write a check. The CEO then tells the freelancer, "Great, now just add real payment processing to the Prototype and launch it to the public tomorrow."

Because the Prototype has no structural foundation, the payments crash, the data is hacked, and the app dies. 

When you engage a premium offshore software agency, they will force you to separate these phases. They will build the Figma Prototype first to test your assumptions. Then, they will build a highly secure MVP to launch your business. Knowing the difference will save you hundreds of thousands of dollars in wasted engineering.
