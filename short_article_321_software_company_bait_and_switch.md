# The "Bait and Switch": A Dangerous Trick Played by Software Development Companies

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** software development company, offshore software agency, custom software development risks

A non-technical CEO is looking for a **software development company** to build a critical internal B2B logistics platform. 

The CEO schedules a Zoom call with a massive, highly-rated agency. On the call, the agency brings their absolute best. The CEO speaks with a charismatic, brilliant "Principal Architect" who has 15 years of experience, two master's degrees, and a flawless understanding of logistics algorithms. 

The CEO is incredibly impressed. The Principal Architect maps out the entire system in 30 minutes. The CEO confidently signs a $200,000 contract. 

Six months later, the software is delivered. It is a catastrophic, unscalable disaster. The code is messy, the database crashes constantly, and the logic is full of amateur mistakes. 

The CEO is furious. How could the brilliant Principal Architect write such terrible code? 

The CEO learns a brutal industry secret: The Principal Architect didn't write a single line of code. The agency executed the classic **"Bait and Switch."**

## The Anatomy of the Bait and Switch
Many massive, factory-style software development companies operate on a highly deceptive business model. 

They employ a tiny, elite strike team of "Sales Architects." These brilliant engineers are paraded in front of clients during the initial sales process to win the contract. They make you feel safe. 

But the exact second the $200,000 contract is signed, the elite Architect vanishes. They are moved to the next sales pitch. 

Who actually writes your code? The agency quietly hands your massive, complex B2B project to a "B-Team" of 22-year-old junior developers who just graduated from a coding bootcamp three months ago. You paid premium rates for a master craftsman, and you received an assembly line of untrained apprentices. 

## How to Defend Your Enterprise
When you are evaluating an offshore software development company, you cannot let yourself be seduced by a single brilliant salesperson. You must contractually secure the actual talent. 

Here are the three mathematical defenses against the Bait and Switch:

### 1. Demand the Resumes of the Exact Team
Before you sign the Master Services Agreement (MSA), say: *"I do not want to see generic agency profiles. I want the specific resumes of the exact 4 human beings who will be typing the code for my project."*

If the agency says, *"We assign developers based on availability after the contract is signed,"* hang up the phone immediately. Elite agencies will explicitly lock down the specific Senior Engineers for your project before you sign. 

### 2. The "Key Personnel" Legal Clause
Have your corporate lawyer insert a **Key Personnel Clause** into the contract. 
This clause mathematically states: *"Developer A and Developer B are designated as Key Personnel. The agency is legally forbidden from removing them from this project without 30 days written notice and explicit approval from the client."*

If the agency refuses to sign this clause, they are planning to switch your team. 

### 3. Interview the Doers, Not the Talkers
During the final stages of the sales process, explicitly request a 30-minute technical interview with the Lead Developer who will be managing the daily code (not the Sales Architect). 

If the Lead Developer cannot hold a high-level conversation about database scaling, CI/CD pipelines, and your specific business logic, you are being handed a junior team. 

Custom software is not built by a logo or a brand name; it is built by human brains. Make sure you know exactly which brains you are paying for before you wire the money to a software development company.
