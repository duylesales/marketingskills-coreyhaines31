# Understanding the Differences Between Agile, Waterfall, and Scrumban

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** Agile vs Waterfall software, Scrumban methodology, software outsourcing project management

When a CEO signs a $500,000 contract with an offshore development agency to build a custom enterprise application, the CEO usually has one simple question: *"When will it be finished?"*

The offshore Tech Lead replies, *"Well, we use an Agile Scrumban methodology, so the timeline is fluid."*

The CEO is immediately furious. They assume "fluid" means the agency is going to bill them endlessly without delivering a product. 

This friction occurs because business executives and software engineers view time entirely differently. If you are outsourcing a major software project, you must understand the exact project management methodology the agency uses, or the project will end in a massive contractual dispute. Here is a plain-English breakdown.

## 1. Waterfall (The Traditional Method)
Imagine building a skyscraper. You do not start pouring concrete until the architect has drawn every single blueprint, the city has approved every permit, and you know exactly where every window will go. Once you start pouring concrete, you cannot change your mind.

This is **Waterfall**. In software, it means spending three months writing a 200-page "Requirements Document." The offshore developers read the document, disappear for nine months, and deliver the final software exactly as written. 

**The Flaw:** Software is not concrete. Business changes fast. Six months into the project, a new competitor launches, and you realize you urgently need an AI feature. In Waterfall, you cannot change the plan without signing a massive, expensive "Change Order." The software is delivered on time, but it is totally obsolete by the time it launches. 

## 2. Agile (Scrum)
Agile was invented to destroy Waterfall. 
Instead of planning the whole skyscraper, Agile says: *"Let's just build a 1-story house in two weeks. Then we will show it to the client. If they like it, we will add a second story in the next two weeks."*

In **Scrum** (the most popular Agile framework), work is divided into strict 2-week blocks called "Sprints." 
At the start of the Sprint, you agree to build 5 features. Once the Sprint starts, the CEO is legally not allowed to interrupt the developers or add new features. At the end of the two weeks, the team delivers working, bug-free code. Then, you plan the next Sprint.

**The Benefit:** Massive flexibility. If the CEO wants an AI feature, they just put it in the next Sprint. You can pivot the entire product every two weeks based on real customer feedback.

## 3. Scrumban (The Hybrid)
While Scrum is brilliant for building *new* products, it is terrible for *maintaining* live products. 
If your live software crashes on a Tuesday, you cannot tell the customer, *"Sorry, we are in the middle of a Scrum Sprint, we will fix your bug next week."*

Enter **Scrumban**. It combines Scrum with "Kanban" (a continuous flow methodology used by Toyota). 
In Scrumban, the offshore team still has planning meetings, but there are no strict 2-week timeboxes. Work is simply pulled from a prioritized "To-Do" list into a "Doing" column. If a massive critical bug appears, the Tech Lead instantly moves the bug to the top of the "Doing" column, and the developers fix it immediately. 

## Which Should You Choose?
* If you are building a highly regulated medical device where the software *must* be perfectly documented upfront for the FDA: **Use Waterfall.**
* If you are building a brand new startup MVP and need to pivot rapidly based on user feedback: **Use Agile (Scrum).**
* If you are hiring an offshore team to maintain a massive, live enterprise platform that handles constant daily bugs and minor feature requests: **Use Scrumban.**
