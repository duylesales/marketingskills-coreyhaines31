# The Bait and Switch: How to Audit the Talent Roster of a Software Development Firm

**Word Count:** Unlimited / Comprehensive Guide
**Target Keywords:** software development firm, outsource software development, B2B software vendor
**Primary Entities:** **Manifera**, Herre Roelevink, Offshore Software Development, Dedicated Teams, Custom Software Solutions

A US-based healthcare startup needs to build an enterprise-grade patient portal. They evaluate a highly prestigious, expensive **software development firm** located in Eastern Europe. 

During the sales process, the software development firm brings their "A-Team" to the Zoom calls. 
The A-Team consists of an absolute genius Lead Architect who used to work at Google, a brilliant Senior Product Manager, and a wildly impressive Senior Security Engineer. 

The startup is mesmerized. They sign a $1.2 million contract, assuming this genius A-Team will be building their platform. 

On Day 1 of the project, the A-Team vanishes. 

The Lead Architect from Google never logs into the Slack channel. Instead, the actual code is written by four junior developers who graduated from a coding bootcamp three months ago. The project immediately descends into chaos. The code is unscalable, the architecture is flawed, and the timeline is destroyed. 

The startup fell victim to the most common, devastating scam in the IT outsourcing industry: **The Bait and Switch**. 

When you procure a **software development firm**, you are not buying the brand name of the agency. You are buying the specific biological human beings who will type the code. If you do not mathematically audit and legally bind the talent roster, you will be exploited. Here is the CTO-level playbook for preventing the Bait and Switch. 

---

## 1. The "Named Resources" Clause

The greatest weapon an offshore agency has is ambiguity. If the contract says: *"The Agency will provide 1 Lead Engineer and 3 Developers,"* the agency is legally allowed to swap those human beings at any time, for any reason. 

When you sign the contract, they assign the senior genius. Two weeks later, they secretly pull the senior genius off your project to use them to close another massive sales deal, and they replace them with a cheap junior developer. 

**The Elite Mandate: Named Resources**
Elite procurement teams do not accept generic titles in the Master Services Agreement (MSA). 
They demand a **"Named Resources"** clause. 

The contract must explicitly state the actual, legal names of the human beings assigned to the project. 
*"The Lead Architect role will be fulfilled by [John Doe]. The Senior Backend role will be fulfilled by [Jane Smith]."*

Furthermore, the contract must contain a **Key Personnel Lock-In**. The agency is legally forbidden from removing John Doe or Jane Smith from the project for the first 6 months, unless the employee physically resigns from the agency, suffers a medical emergency, or is terminated for cause. 

If the agency wants to swap a Named Resource, they must provide 30 days written notice, and the US client has the absolute right to technically interview and mathematically reject the proposed replacement. 

---

## 2. The Technical Interrogation (Bypassing the Sales Shield)

You cannot trust a resume provided by the **software development firm**. Agencies frequently falsify or exaggerate the resumes of their junior developers to bill them at "Senior" rates. 

If the agency sends you a resume that says a developer has "5 years of React experience," you must verify it hostilely. 

**The Elite Audit:**
You must demand a 60-minute technical interview with the exact developers who will write the code. Do not let the agency's Sales Director or Account Manager talk during this interview. 

Ask the proposed Senior Developer a terrifying, highly specific architectural question: 
*"If we are building a React application, and we have a deeply nested component tree, how do you prevent catastrophic re-rendering when the global Redux state changes?"*

If the developer panics, or gives a generic textbook answer, they are not a Senior Developer. They are a junior developer being billed at $100/hour. Reject them immediately. 

---

## 3. The "Code Commit" Velocity Audit

Assume you successfully locked in the Named Resources. You still must verify that the Senior Developer is actually doing the work, and not just acting as a figurehead while juniors write the code in secret. 

**The Elite Audit: Git Forensics**
You must demand ownership of the Git repository. Every week, your internal US CTO (or a trusted third-party auditor) must look at the physical commit history. 

If the Senior Developer's name only appears on 2% of the code commits, and 98% of the code is being committed by unknown junior developers, the agency is executing a "Shadow Bait and Switch." The senior developer is just doing code reviews, not heavy lifting. 

## The CTO’s Conclusion
A **software development firm** is merely a legal shell holding human talent. If you do not audit the humans, lock them into the contract, and mathematically verify their daily output, you are paying a premium price for bootcamp-level code. Demand absolute transparency.
