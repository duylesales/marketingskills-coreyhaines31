A US logistics company hires a 15-person **dedicated development team** in Eastern Europe to build and maintain their complex freight management software. 

The offshore team is highly productive. They are constantly deploying new features. 

However, the US Operations Manager notices a problem. Three weeks ago, they submitted a bug report: *"The invoice PDF generator occasionally prints the total amount in the wrong font size."*

The Operations Manager put the bug in Jira. They set the priority to "Medium." 

Three weeks pass. The bug is not fixed. 
The Operations Manager asks the offshore Lead Developer: *"Why haven't you fixed the invoice bug?"*
The Lead Developer replies: *"We are focused on the 'New Route Optimization' Epic that the CEO demanded. We haven't had time for the bug."*

The Operations Manager submits another bug: *"The login screen is freezing for 2 seconds on Android."*
Four weeks pass. It is not fixed. 

Slowly, the Jira backlog fills up with 200 unfixed bugs. The software is technically "getting new features," but it feels clunky, unstable, and frustrating to use. The US company is furious, but the offshore team claims they are working at maximum capacity. 

The US company fell victim to the **Jira Ticket Black Hole**. 

When you manage a **dedicated development team**, if you do not architect a mathematical system for Bug Prioritization, the offshore team will always choose to build "fun new features" instead of fixing "boring old bugs." Here is the CTO-level playbook for enforcing bug resolution.

## 1. The Physics of the "Feature Bias"

Why does the offshore team ignore bugs? 

Because of the physics of engineering incentives. 

Building a new feature (like "Route Optimization") is intellectually stimulating for a software engineer. It requires creativity and architecture. Furthermore, building new features makes the CEO happy. 

Fixing a bug (like a wrong font size) is incredibly tedious. It requires the developer to read hundreds of lines of old code, hunt for a tiny mathematical error, and fix it. It is boring. And no CEO throws a party because you fixed a font size. 

If you give a **dedicated development team** a choice between a Feature and a Bug, they will mathematically choose the Feature 100% of the time. The bugs will rot at the bottom of the Jira backlog forever. 

---

## 2. The Elite Architecture: The "Zero Bug Policy"

You cannot ask the offshore team to "try to fix bugs when you have time." They will never have time. You must architect a structural mandate. 

**The Elite Mandate: The 20% Bug Tax**
Elite US CTOs mathematically force bug resolution into the Agile Sprint architecture. 

During Sprint Planning, the US CTO dictates: *"Every 2-week Sprint must dedicate exactly 20% of its Story Points to fixing bugs. This is non-negotiable."*

If the offshore team has a total capacity of 100 Story Points, they are legally forbidden from allocating more than 80 points to new features. The remaining 20 points MUST be spent pulling bugs out of the Jira Black Hole. 

By taxing the feature development, you mathematically guarantee that the software's stability improves every single Sprint. 

---

## 3. The "Service Level Agreement (SLA)" Matrix

Not all bugs are equal. The offshore team often ignores bugs because the US team fails to prioritize them correctly. If every bug is marked "High Priority," then nothing is High Priority. 

**The Elite Architecture: The Defect SLA Matrix**
When you manage a **dedicated development team**, the US CTO must enforce a mathematical SLA Matrix for Jira tickets. 

* **P0 (Critical/Blocker):** The production server is down. Users cannot log in. 
  * *SLA Mandate:* The offshore team drops all feature work immediately. They must respond in 15 minutes and fix it within 4 hours. 
* **P1 (High):** A major function is broken (e.g., checkout is failing for 10% of users), but a temporary workaround exists. 
  * *SLA Mandate:* The offshore team must fix this bug within the current 2-week Sprint. No excuses. 
* **P2 (Medium):** A minor UI glitch (e.g., wrong font size). 
  * *SLA Mandate:* This bug enters the backlog and must be resolved within 3 Sprints using the 20% Bug Tax. 
* **P3 (Low):** A typo in the Terms of Service. 
  * *SLA Mandate:* Resolved whenever convenient. 

If the US Product Manager submits a bug, they MUST assign it a P0-P3 grade. If they assign P1, the offshore team mathematically knows they must halt minor feature work to hit the SLA. 

## The CTO’s Mandate
A Jira backlog filled with 200 rotting bugs is proof of architectural failure. When you manage a **dedicated development team**, you cannot rely on the developers' goodwill to fix boring problems. You must mandate structural enforcement. Implement the 20% Bug Tax in every Sprint. Enforce a rigid, mathematically defined P0-P3 SLA Matrix. Transform your Agile process from a "Feature Factory" into a balanced, highly disciplined engineering machine that values stability as much as velocity.
