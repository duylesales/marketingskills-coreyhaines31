# Why Agile Development Fails When Outsourced Incorrectly

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** agile software development offshore, outsource agile methodology, custom software project management

A mid-sized logistics company decides to build a custom inventory dashboard. The CEO reads an article about "Agile Methodology" and decides this is the only way to build software. 

The CEO hires a cheap offshore agency in Eastern Europe and says: *"We are going to use Agile! No strict requirements. We will just collaborate, iterate every two weeks, and adapt to change!"*

The offshore team enthusiastically agrees. 

Three months later, the CEO expects a working prototype. The offshore team delivers a chaotic mess. The login screen is half-finished, the database is completely unorganized, and they have burned through $50,000. 

The CEO screams: *"Why is nothing finished?!"*
The offshore Lead Developer replies: *"Because you are doing Agile! You change your mind every three days! We keep throwing away code to 'adapt to change'!"*

Agile is a brilliant framework when an internal engineering team sits in the same room as the CEO. But when Agile is outsourced across 8,000 miles, 5 time zones, and a massive cultural barrier, the lack of structure is a recipe for catastrophic financial failure. Here is how to do Offshore Agile correctly. 

## The Myth of "No Requirements"
Amateur founders love Agile because they think it means they don't have to do any hard work upfront. They think they can just hire developers and figure out the product "on the fly." 

When you outsource, time is literally money. If you figure out the product "on the fly," you are paying offshore developers $50 an hour to sit around and wait for you to make a decision. 

Elite offshore agencies refuse to start an Agile project until a **Sprint 0 (Discovery Phase)** is completed. 
During Sprint 0, no code is written. The offshore Product Manager sits with the CEO and ruthlessly defines the absolute minimum features required for the MVP (Minimum Viable Product). They draw the wireframes. They architect the database. 

Only after the foundational blueprint is locked do they begin the "Agile" coding process. You can change the color of the buttons later, but you cannot change the foundation of the house after the concrete is poured. 

## The Rigid Mathematics of the "Sprint"
Agile does not mean chaos. It means working in highly disciplined, 14-day cycles called Sprints. 

If you are outsourcing, you must enforce the **Iron Law of the Sprint Backlog**. 
On Monday morning, the CEO and the offshore team agree: *"In this 14-day Sprint, we will build the Login Screen and the Checkout Cart."*

On Wednesday, the CEO has a brilliant new idea: *"Forget the Checkout Cart! Build a Chatbot!"*

In a cheap, chaotic offshore agency, the developer will say "Okay," delete the Checkout code, and start the Chatbot. 
In a premium, elite offshore agency, the Technical Project Manager will aggressively say: **"NO."**

The TPM will tell the CEO: *"We committed to the Checkout Cart. The Sprint is locked. We are not touching the Chatbot until the next Sprint begins in 10 days."*

This rigid discipline is the only thing that protects your budget from your own chaotic ideas. 

## The Mandatory Daily Standup
Agile only works if communication is flawless. If you are sleeping in New York while the team is coding in Vietnam, you cannot rely on occasional Slack messages. 

You must mandate a **Daily Standup**. Every single day, at a time that overlaps both time zones (e.g., 9:00 PM in NY / 8:00 AM in Vietnam), the CEO and the offshore team jump on a strict, 15-minute Zoom call. 
Everyone answers three questions:
1. What did you build yesterday?
2. What are you building today?
3. What is blocking you from working?

If you try to outsource Agile without strict Discovery, a locked Sprint Backlog, and daily vocal communication, you are not doing Agile. You are just paying strangers to write random code until your budget runs out.
