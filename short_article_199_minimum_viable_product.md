# What is an MVP (Minimum Viable Product) and How to Decide Which Features Make the Cut

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** software MVP development, offshore MVP strategy, custom software feature scoping

A visionary founder comes up with a billion-dollar idea for a new social fitness app. 

They write a 40-page document outlining every feature the app must have: a 3D avatar creator, a cryptocurrency rewards system, AI-powered workout generation, global leaderboards, and an integrated e-commerce store to buy protein powder. 

They take this massive document to an offshore software agency. The agency quotes $800,000 and two years of development time. 

The founder spends the $800,000. Two years later, the app launches. And then, a brutal reality hits: Nobody downloads it. Users do not care about the 3D avatars. They just wanted a simple app to log their daily runs, but the core tracking feature is clunky because the developers spent all their time building the cryptocurrency store. 

The founder just burned $800,000 building features nobody wanted. 

This is why the greatest software companies in the world do not launch massive platforms on Day 1. They launch an **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)**. If you are outsourcing software development, strictly scoping your MVP is the only way to protect your capital.

## What is an MVP?
An MVP is the absolute ugliest, smallest, bare-bones version of your software that still solves the core problem for the user. 

If your goal is to build a beautiful, customized Ferrari (the final software), you do not start by spending three years building a Ferrari engine. 
* Your MVP is a skateboard. 
* Version 2 is a bicycle. 
* Version 3 is a motorcycle. 
* Version 4 is the Ferrari. 

The skateboard is ugly, but it proves the core hypothesis: *Will people use a wheeled vehicle to go from Point A to Point B?* 

## How to Ruthlessly Cut Features
When scoping an MVP with your offshore Product Manager, you must engage in ruthless feature prioritization. Use the **MoSCoW Method**:

### 1. MUST Have (The Core Engine)
These are the non-negotiable features. Without them, the software physically cannot function or is legally non-compliant. 
*Example for the Fitness App:* User Login, GPS Run Tracking, Save Data to Database. 
*If a feature is not a "Must Have," it is banned from the MVP.*

### 2. SHOULD Have (Important but Not Vital)
These features add immense value but are not strictly required for the app to function on Day 1. 
*Example:* The ability to share your run on Twitter. 
*Action:* Push this to Version 2 (Month 3 of development).

### 3. COULD Have (The "Nice to Haves")
These are luxury features that look cool but don't fundamentally solve the core business problem. 
*Example:* The 3D avatar creator. 
*Action:* Push this to Version 3 (Year 2 of development).

### 4. WON'T Have (The Distractions)
These are features that sound amazing in a pitch deck but are completely outside the core business model. 
*Example:* The integrated cryptocurrency protein powder store. 
*Action:* Delete it entirely from the roadmap until the app has 1 million active users.

## The Financial Power of the MVP
By ruthlessly cutting the "Should," "Could," and "Won't" features, your offshore agency can launch the MVP in just three months for $50,000, instead of two years and $800,000. 

You launch the MVP to real users. The users will immediately tell you what is missing. They might say, *"We don't care about avatars, but we desperately want an Apple Watch integration."*

You now take your remaining $750,000 budget and pay the offshore team to build exactly what the market demanded. You eliminate all financial risk by letting real user data, not executive guesswork, dictate the software engineering roadmap.
