# How to Write a Software RFP (Request for Proposal)

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** software RFP, request for proposal, software outsourcing process

When a company decides to outsource a custom software project, the first step is usually sending a **Request for Proposal (RFP)** to several different development agencies. 

An RFP is a document that explains what you want to build, so agencies can reply with a timeline and a price quote. 

Unfortunately, 90% of software RFPs are written so poorly that reputable agencies simply ignore them. If you send out a vague RFP, you will only attract desperate, low-quality agencies who will promise you the world and deliver nothing. Here is how to write an RFP that attracts elite software engineering teams.

## 1. State the Business Problem, Not Just Features
Do not start your RFP with a list of 50 buttons you want on a screen. Elite software architects need to understand the *context*.
Start with the "Why." 
* *Bad:* "We need a mobile app with a map and a chat feature."
* *Good:* "We are a regional plumbing company losing 20% of our daily appointments because our plumbers get lost or cannot find parking. We need a dispatch app to reduce missed appointments."
When developers know the business goal, they can suggest technical solutions you might never have thought of.

## 2. Be Explicit About Your Current Tech Stack
If you are building a new application that needs to talk to your existing databases, the agency must know what those databases are.
List your current infrastructure: "We currently run on AWS. Our CRM is Salesforce. Our main database is PostgreSQL." If you hide this information, the agency cannot accurately price the API integration work.

## 3. Define the Budget Range
Many companies hide their budget in an RFP, hoping an agency will magically bid a super low number. This is a massive waste of time. 
If your budget is $30,000, you need a completely different technical approach than if your budget is $300,000. By stating, "Our budget for Phase 1 is between $50k and $70k," you allow the agency to design an architecture (like using cross-platform tools instead of native development) that actually fits your wallet.

## 4. Ask for the Team's Resumes, Not Just the Company Portfolio
A common mistake is reviewing the agency's beautiful portfolio and assuming that exact level of quality will apply to your project. 
The reality is that an agency might have 200 developers. You need to know who is working on *your* specific project. In the RFP, ask the agency to provide anonymized CVs of the exact Tech Lead, Product Manager, and Senior Developers who will be assigned to your pod. 

## 5. Demand an Agile Proposal
If an agency replies to your RFP saying, "We will deliver the finished software in 8 months for exactly $150,000," throw their proposal in the trash. That is a Waterfall lie. 
Ask agencies to define their Agile process in their response. How often will they show you working code? How do they handle changes in scope? How do they structure their 2-week sprints? 

A rigorous, well-written RFP protects your company and guarantees you attract a highly professional, transparent outsourcing partner.
