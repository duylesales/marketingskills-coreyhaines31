# Why Your Software Project Needs a Product Manager (Not Just Developers)

**Word Count:** ~600 words
**Target Keywords:** software product manager, product management, software development roles

When a company decides to outsource a software project, they usually hyper-focus on hiring coders. They want senior React developers, elite Python architects, and mobile experts. 

However, one of the most common reasons outsourced software projects fail is that the client refuses to pay for a **Software Product Manager (PM)**. They assume that if they just hand a list of features to the developers, the developers will figure it out.

This is a massive mistake. Here is why the Product Manager is the most critical role on a software development team.

## Developers Speak "Code," You Speak "Business"
Imagine hiring a team of bricklayers to build a house, but refusing to hire a general contractor or an architect. You just walk onto the dirt lot and tell the bricklayers, "I want a three-bedroom house." The house will be a disaster.

Software developers are highly specialized craftsmen. Their job is to write logical, efficient, and secure code based on specific technical instructions. 
Your job, as a business leader, is to maximize revenue and user adoption.

These are two entirely different languages. If you tell a developer, "Make the checkout process better," the developer will likely rewrite the database queries to make the page load 0.2 seconds faster. But what you actually meant was, "Add Apple Pay so users don't abandon their carts."

## The PM is the Translator
The Product Manager acts as the critical bridge between business strategy and technical execution. 

A great PM will sit down with you and understand your ultimate business goal (e.g., "We need to reduce cart abandonment by 15%"). 
The PM will then translate that business goal into highly specific technical "User Stories" and Jira tickets for the developers. 
* *Ticket 1: Integrate Apple Pay API.*
* *Ticket 2: Move the 'Guest Checkout' button above the fold.*
* *Ticket 3: Implement an email trigger for abandoned carts.*

The developers do not have to guess what you want; they just execute the clear roadmap provided by the PM.

## The Master of Scope Creep
During any software project, stakeholders will constantly suggest new features. "Wouldn't it be cool if the app also had a chat feature?" 

If you ask a developer to build a chat feature, they will simply start building it, completely blowing up your timeline and budget. 
The Product Manager's primary job is to aggressively defend the scope of the project. They are the ones who say, "A chat feature is a great idea, but it will add three weeks to the timeline and $15,000 to the budget. Let's put it on the backlog for Phase 2 and focus on launching the MVP first."

## Don't Cut Corners on Management
When reviewing a proposal from a software agency, do not ask them to remove the Product Manager to save 10% on the bill. A good PM will save you 40% in wasted developer hours by ensuring that the engineers are always building the right thing, in the right order. 

If you are building an offshore dedicated team, ensuring that a seasoned, English-fluent Product Manager leads the pod is the absolute guarantee of a successful product launch.
